1The Three Surfaces

First, the question almost everyone asks: "Wait, isn't this just Claude?" Yes and no. Same brain underneath. Three very different surfaces though, and only one of them is built to be your COS.

Claude Chat

claude.ai (browser)

A brilliant intern with amnesia.

  • Great at one-off questions
  • Writes, brainstorms, explains
  • Forgets you between sessions
  • Can't touch your files
  • Can't act on your tools

Cowork (Claude in Chrome)

browser extension

A remote assistant who can drive your browser.

  • Clicks through web apps for you
  • Useful when there's no API
  • Slow (it's clicking buttons)
  • Breaks when UIs change
  • Still no persistent memory
Your COS

Claude Code

desktop app

A teammate that lives on your machine, remembers everything, and actually acts.

  • Reads & writes your files
  • Persistent memory across sessions
  • Plugged into Monday, Slack, Drive, etc.
  • Runs your saved playbooks (skills)
  • Builds you new tools on demand

Important: this isn't a downgrade or upgrade across the three. They're different surfaces for different jobs. Chat is great for a quick gut-check on the road. Cowork is the right answer when you need to click through a web app that has no API. Claude Code is what becomes your Chief of Staff because of the three things only it has: files, memory, and tools.

Under the hood: why these three exist at all

Same model (Claude), three different runtimes. Chat is the original consumer product (cloud-hosted, sandboxed, no machine access). Cowork ships a browser extension that lets the model take screenshots and synthesize clicks — useful for legacy systems without APIs. Claude Code is the developer-grade runtime: it runs locally, reads your filesystem, executes shell commands, and connects to outside tools via the Model Context Protocol (MCP).

Why does it have to be a different surface? Because giving an AI access to your files and your real production tools needs a stronger trust model than a browser tab. Claude Code asks permission for everything sensitive — you can see and approve each action before it runs.

2Anatomy of Your COS

Your COS is just a folder on your laptop. That's it. The intelligence is the model. But the memory, the instructions, the playbooks, the project files — that's all yours, living locally, version-controlled, never shared.

Here's what's in the folder: 

~/Desktop/EMC Projects/
├── CLAUDE.md                  ← your instruction sheet. Read first every session.
├── memory/                    ← sticky notes that survive across sessions
│   ├── MEMORY.md                the index (one line per memory)
│   ├── user_*.md                who you are, how you work
│   ├── feedback_*.md            corrections + validated approaches
│   ├── project_*.md             active initiatives + deadlines
│   └── reference_*.md           where to look in external systems
├── .claude/                   ← settings, hooks, permissions
│   ├── settings.json            what's allowed without prompting
│   └── skills/                  your saved playbooks (see Section 4)
└── Your Project Folders/      ← actual working files
    ├── Q2-2026 ...              current quarter's work
    └── Supporting Projects/     ongoing topical work

Two things to take away from this picture:

One: CLAUDE.md is the contract. Every time you open your COS, that file is the first thing it reads. It's where you write down who you are, what matters this quarter, who reports to you, and how you like to be talked to. As things change, you change that file. Your COS adapts immediately.

Two: the memory/ folder is what makes your COS get smarter over time. We'll dig into how it works in the next section. For now: every time your COS learns something durable about you, it writes a small file. Next session, it reads them. Compound interest, but for context.

Under the hood: settings, hooks, and permissions

.claude/settings.json tells the COS what it can do without asking permission every time. Reading files? Always fine. Posting to a Monday pulse? Configurable per person. Sending a Slack message to a customer? Always asks. You set the trust ladder where you want it.

Hooks are automated behaviors that fire on events. Example: every time a session ends, run a script that backs up your CLAUDE.md to a private Git repo. Example two: every time you mention a new person, append them to a "people I need to remember" file. You don't have to set these up day one — most of us added ours over time as we noticed patterns we wanted automated.

Permissions are the safety layer on top. Even if a setting allows something, the COS still surfaces big or irreversible actions to you for approval. Deleting files, sending external messages, pushing to GitHub — those all stop and wait.

3Memory

Here's why your COS gets sharper the longer you work with it: it writes things down. Every time it learns something durable — a correction you made, a preference you mentioned, a project deadline that matters — it drops a memory file. Next session, it reads those first.

Four types. Each one has a job.

User

Who you are, how you work. Your role, your preferences, your communication style. Rarely changes.

Feedback

Corrections you've made and approaches you've validated. So your COS doesn't make the same mistake twice and doesn't drift away from things that worked.

Project

Active initiatives, deadlines, decisions. The fastest-changing tier — your COS keeps it current.

Reference

Where to look in external systems. "Bug reports are in the Linear INGEST project." "Oncall watches this Grafana board."

A real entry from Albert's memory, so you can see the format:

memory/feedback_monday_no_cdata.md Rule: Never wrap Monday update bodies in <![CDATA[ ... ]]>. The HTML body renders empty when wrapped. Why: First time I posted a recap, the update came through blank with only the @-mention. Plain HTML in the body field works. CDATA is for XML; Monday's update body is JSON. How to apply: Any time I'm calling create_update, pass HTML directly. Skip the wrapper.

Notice the shape: the rule, the why, and how to apply it. That third part is what turns a memory from a parrot into a teammate. Knowing why lets your COS judge edge cases instead of just blindly following the rule.

The index file (MEMORY.md) is one line per memory. It loads at the top of every session, so your COS always knows what it could pull. The detail files load only when relevant. That's how the system stays fast even with hundreds of memories.

The compound effect. Week one, your COS knows what's in CLAUDE.md. Week four, it's also learned 20 of your preferences. Month three, it's got hundreds of memories and it stops feeling like a tool and starts feeling like someone who's been working with you for a while. That's not magic. That's just the memory file growing.

Skills are how your COS scales. I've got 40+ today. Most of them I built because I noticed I was doing the same kind of task three times in a row. Now I just type the slash command and the COS does it the way I want it done. The first one you build will feel like a real unlock.

5MCPs & APIs

MCP stands for Model Context Protocol. Plain version: it's how your COS plugs into the apps you already use. When you ask your COS to post an update on a pulse, it doesn't open Monday in a browser. It talks to Monday's API directly. Milliseconds.

Each connection unlocks a different kind of work:

Triage pulses, post updates, run weekly velocity, move work between groups.

Slack

Read message threads, draft replies, scan channels for things worth surfacing. Sends only with your approval.

Google Drive

Create files, share with the right people, search across folders, upload polished decks.

Triage your inbox, draft replies in your voice, search threads from months back.

Dialpad

Grade sales calls, surface coaching moments, pull transcripts.

Samsara

Pull driver scores, audit unsigned warnings, surface safety trends across branches.

ClearCo

Hiring funnel data — applicants, screens, interviews, hires. Live once API access lands.

Under the hood: MCPs vs raw APIs, and when each is right

MCP is a standard wrapper that makes any tool look the same to the AI. Instead of writing custom code for every integration, you install an MCP server for Monday, an MCP server for Slack, etc. — and the COS picks them up automatically with a consistent interface. Easier to add new tools, easier to share configs across people.

Raw APIs (calling the service directly with curl or a Python script) are still the right answer when (a) the MCP server doesn't exist yet, (b) you need a specific feature the MCP doesn't expose, or (c) the MCP is misbehaving and you want to bypass it for a one-off. We use both. MCP is the default, raw API is the escape hatch.

One quirk worth knowing: MCPs are still early-days software. They drop connections sometimes. When that happens, your COS knows to fall back to raw API calls — you usually don't notice.

6The Network

Your COS isn't alone. Four are live across Einstein leadership today: Albert (mine), Gerald (Mike's), Matisen's COS, and Clark (Paul's). Three more joining this quarter — you'll be one of them.

They talk to each other through the Shared Context Board — a Monday board they can all see and post to. When my Albert needs something from Mike's Gerald, it posts a note. Gerald picks it up next session and replies. Both of us stay in the loop.

Live today Joining this quarter

Worked example. Last month I needed a current Samsara breakdown to plan a pricing conversation. Albert posted a one-line ask to the Shared Context Board tagged for Gerald. Gerald saw it the next time Mike ran a session, pulled the data from his Samsara MCP (which mine doesn't have), wrote up a reply, and posted it back. Both Mike and I saw the exchange and could chime in. No Slack thread, no calendar invite, no waiting for a sync.

Why this matters. It's how a fleet of COSes coordinates without humans manually relaying every handoff. The board is the comms layer. Each leader still owns their own COS and their own context — but the agents themselves can stay in sync on shared work.

Every COS pulls from the same public template at github.com/einsteinadmin/ai-chief-of-staff. So when someone improves a pattern — a better skill, a smarter hook, a cleaner setup — it propagates. You benefit from upgrades the other four agents got last week.

8The Daily Rhythm

Of all the sections in here, this one rewards consistency the most. The system isn't magic — it's a feedback loop. The skills below are how the loop closes. Skip them now and then (a QGR day, a vacation, a heads-down sprint) and you'll be fine. Skip them for more than a few days in a row and the COS starts to drift: memory files go stale, the Shared Context Board piles up, board sanity slides, Kaizen never lands.

Personalize the specifics. I'm describing how I run this rhythm. Yours will look different in places — your boards, your sync cadence, even how you track praise (I run mine through Pending/Delivered sub-items on a dedicated Praise board, but that's my pattern, not the template). The shape is what matters: a morning briefing, an end-of-day close, a Friday system pass. The specifics are yours to figure out, and your COS will help codify them in memory as you go.

Every morning: /gm

Run /gm first thing, most days. Before Monday, before Slack, before email. The COS pulls your calendar, scans your boards, checks notifications, reads new SCB posts from other agents, and writes you a one-page day plan. That's how it learns what changed overnight. Skip when you're going straight into something all-consuming (a QGR session, a deep work block, a travel day). Don't skip three or four days in a row — the morning picture stops being accurate.

End of day: /wrap (light)

Daily /wrap is your close-out. Lighter than the Friday version. Two jobs:
  1. Final save of the day's work into the right pulses, so nothing you touched today goes unlogged.
  2. Board sanity check — Today group cleared, This Week refreshed for tomorrow, anything urgent flagged.

Catches the stuff you meant to log but didn't. Takes 3-5 minutes. Sets you up to start tomorrow with /gm from a clean picture.

Every Friday: /wrap (full)

Friday is the system maintenance pass. This is the heaviest skill in the rotation, and it's where the system maintains itself. Five jobs in one shot:
  1. Logs weekly velocity (every Done pulse, every LOW point — the COS tallies your output)
  2. Surfaces the praise queue so wins don't get lost (I track mine through sub-items on a dedicated Praise board; your pattern might look different)
  3. Scans the Shared Context Board for replies from other COSes you owe
  4. Runs a Kaizen pass — one improvement to the system itself
  5. Prunes memory files that went stale this week

Skip Friday /wrap for more than a couple weeks in a row and the system gets noticeably draggy. Daily wrap is a habit. Friday wrap is the housekeeping that keeps the rest fast.

Situational: /save

Run /save when you're switching contexts mid-day — finishing a focus block, jumping from one project to another, walking away for an extended interruption. It writes a "pick up here" note so the next session (or the next you) starts where this one ended. Think of it as a verbal handoff to your future self.

Don't overuse it. Three or four times in a long workday, max. If you find yourself running it after every five-minute task, that's a signal you're switching contexts too often, not that you need more saves.

Situational: /compact

Different beast. /compact is a memory-management operation, not a handoff. When a session gets long and the conversation history starts filling up the context window, the COS rewrites that history into a tight summary — keeping the important stuff, dropping the noise. The session continues from the compacted history.

Usually you don't have to call this. Autocompact is on and kicks in automatically when things get heavy. But on big work sessions where you want to extend the runway deliberately, typing /compact gives you that fresh slate without losing the thread.

The honest version. Week one, you'll forget /gm half the time. That's fine, build the habit. The leader who runs the rhythm most days for a full quarter ends up with a COS that's noticeably sharper than the leader who runs them sometimes. Same model. Same setup. Different rhythm. The compound effect is the whole game.

9Your Personal Dashboard

Here's what happens when you run those skills regularly: a personal dashboard emerges. Not because you built it. Because everything you're doing is being captured, and the COS can pull it together on demand.

My dashboard lives at qgr.einsteinmoving.com (gated to @einsteinmoving.com) and there's also a lighter-weight live build in the goals-dashboard repo. One HTML page. It shows:

My QGs, current status

Every quarterly goal, green / super-green / red, with the why behind the score.

My directs' QGs

Roundtable rollup — who's on track, who needs a conversation, what changed since last week.

Recent decisions

The calls I've made this quarter and the reasoning, so I don't re-litigate.

Praise queue

Wins that haven't been delivered yet. Surfaces during /gm + /wrap.

Here's the part most people miss: I don't update this dashboard manually. It updates from the skills I'm already running.

/meeting-prepLatest QG status pulled into the prep doc, so I walk in knowing the state
/digestPulses get touched, decisions get logged, dashboard reflects what was just decided
/retroWeekly summary writes a new "what changed this week" entry
/wrapVelocity rolls in, praise queue updates, deadlines re-sort
The build tip. Don't try to design your dashboard up front. Ask your COS to build you a v1 sometime in your first month, using the data it already has on you. Six status cards, a praise strip, a deadline rail. Run it for two weeks. Then tell your COS what's missing and what's noise. Three or four iterations and you'll have something that fits how you actually work.

The dashboard is where the compound effect becomes visible. After a quarter of running the daily rhythm, you can open one URL and see your whole operation — what's green, what's red, what needs a hard conversation this week, who deserves praise, what's slipping. Without it, that picture lives in your head and in Monday and in Slack and in your inbox and in your gut. With it, it's just on a page.

10Tools You'll Need

Your COS does most of the heavy lifting. But it can only work with what you give it. Two categories of outside tools pay for themselves the first week you use them.

Dictation — get your voice into the COS faster than you can type

Why it matters

Most of the prompts you give your COS aren't carefully composed — they're how you actually talk. Dictation captures that authentic voice (and the thinking you do walking the office or driving) without forcing you back to a keyboard. It also turns out the COS responds better to a natural spoken brief than a typed bullet list. You explain more, you assume less.

What I use today

Wispr Flow. Runs in the background, transcribes when I hold a hotkey. Punctuation and code blocks come out clean. Worth it on day one.

AI Notetaker — every meeting becomes a transcript your COS can digest

Why it matters

The /digest skill is one of the highest-value workflows in the COS. You hand it a meeting transcript, it updates the right pulses, logs decisions, surfaces praise, and writes a recap. But it needs a transcript. Without an AI notetaker capturing your meetings, /digest doesn't have anything to work with.

What Mike + I use today

Plaud. Small hardware recorder that captures any in-person or remote meeting and produces a clean transcript. Good for our in-the-field workflow.

Other options worth knowing

Granola (software-only, runs in your menu bar, great for Zoom-heavy calendars). Otter (the classic — solid transcripts, integrates everywhere). Both work fine with /digest.

Standard TBD. I'm working through what the company-wide standard looks like for both categories (dictation + notetaker) — what we license, who pays, how onboarding works. Until that lands, run with what fits your workflow today. The COS works with any of these tools, so you're not locked in. I'll update this section once procurement decisions are final.
